\begin{slidenote}{AI-Guided Prediction of Thermoelastic Wave Propagation in Geological Media}
\Purpose
Open the defence, name the topic, and immediately set the correct research scope.

\WhatToSay
Good morning. The topic of our diploma work is \textit{AI-Guided Prediction of Thermoelastic Wave Propagation in Geological Media}. The work studies how machine learning can support directional prediction for thermoelastic wave behavior in simplified geological media. I would like to emphasize the scope from the beginning: this is a research prototype and a comparative framework. It is not presented as a site-calibrated simulator. The focus is on physical interpretation, model comparison, and estimating directional characteristics under simplified assumptions.

\KeyPoints
\begin{itemize}
  \item The work connects thermoelastic physics, geological parameters, and neural surrogate models.
  \item The main target is directional prediction between a source and a probe point.
  \item The presentation treats the system as a research prototype.
\end{itemize}

\Transition
I will start by explaining why this topic is relevant.
\end{slidenote}

\begin{slidenote}{Relevance of the Topic}
\Purpose
Explain why thermoelastic wave prediction in rocks is worth studying.

\WhatToSay
Thermally affected rocks appear in several applied contexts: geothermal energy, underground construction, thermal stimulation, and geophysical interpretation. When temperature changes inside a rock, it can influence stress, deformation, and wave behavior. A direct coupled numerical simulation is useful, but it can be expensive when many materials, source positions, or observation directions must be compared. This motivates an AI-guided approach, where neural models estimate selected directional responses and help compare scenarios faster. The important point is that the AI model supports the analysis; it does not replace the physical problem formulation.

\KeyPoints
\begin{itemize}
  \item Temperature changes can create mechanical effects in geological media.
  \item Repeated coupled simulations are expensive for comparative studies.
  \item The work uses AI-guided prediction as a supporting research tool.
\end{itemize}

\Transition
This relevance leads to the main research problem.
\end{slidenote}

\begin{slidenote}{Research Problem}
\Purpose
State the central question and narrow it to a defensible bachelor thesis scope.

\WhatToSay
The central question is how to estimate directional characteristics of thermoelastic wave propagation without rerunning a full coupled numerical simulation for every new scenario. Real geological media are complex: they may be porous, fractured, anisotropic, and saturated with fluids. Modelling all these effects is outside the scope of this thesis. Therefore, the work uses a simplified two-dimensional effective-medium formulation. Within that controlled setting, the prototype compares several neural surrogate approaches for directional prediction.

\KeyPoints
\begin{itemize}
  \item The problem is framed as directional prediction, not full field simulation.
  \item Real rocks are more complex than the simplified model.
  \item The simplified setup makes controlled model comparison possible.
\end{itemize}

\KeyPoints
\begin{itemize}

  \item The models are compared under one shared prediction contract.

\begin{slidenote}{Thermoelastic Coupling Logic}
\Purpose
Introduce the physical chain from heating to wave-like mechanical response.

\WhatToSay
This slide shows the basic logic of thermoelastic coupling. A local thermal source changes the temperature field. Temperature change produces thermal expansion, and if the expansion is constrained by the surrounding material, stress appears. Stress gradients then create mechanical displacement, and this response propagates through the medium. The formula at the bottom is not meant to be read as a full equation. It summarizes the chain: temperature leads to thermal strain, thermal strain contributes to stress, and stress drives displacement.

\KeyPoints
\begin{itemize}
  \item Heating changes the temperature field.
  \item Thermal expansion can produce stress.
  \item Stress gradients drive displacement and propagation.
\end{itemize}

\begin{slidenote}{Rock Properties Controlling Propagation}
\Purpose
Explain the physical role of mechanical and thermal rock properties.

\WhatToSay
Rock properties influence propagation through both mechanical and thermal mechanisms. Density controls inertia: a denser medium resists acceleration more strongly. Elastic stiffness controls how strongly the rock resists deformation. Porosity, cracks, and saturation can reduce effective stiffness and change wave behavior. On the thermal side, conductivity controls heat transfer, heat capacity controls thermal storage, and thermal expansion controls how temperature change becomes strain. In real rocks, bedding, foliation, aligned pores, fractures, and stress state can also create directional effects.

\KeyPoints
\begin{itemize}
  \item Mechanical properties control inertia and stiffness.
  \item Thermal properties control heat spreading and thermal strain.
  \item Geological structure can create directional behavior.
\end{itemize}

\begin{slidenote}{Elastic Waves in Geological Media}
\Purpose
Introduce compressional and shear waves using simple physical interpretation.

\WhatToSay
Elastic wave propagation is commonly described using P-waves and S-waves. P-waves are compressional waves, where particle motion is mainly parallel to the propagation direction. S-waves are shear waves, where particle motion is transverse and depends on shear rigidity. The formulas show that wave velocity depends on stiffness and density. Physically, a stiffer rock usually transmits disturbances faster, while porosity and compliant cracks often reduce effective velocity. These relations also justify using wave speed as a reference for travel-time sanity checks.

\KeyPoints
\begin{itemize}
  \item P-waves involve compression and volume change.
  \item S-waves depend on shear rigidity.
  \item Wave velocities depend on elastic moduli and density.
\end{itemize}

\begin{slidenote}{Heat Conduction and Thermal Expansion}
\Purpose
Explain how heat transfer enters the thermoelastic formulation.

\WhatToSay
The thermal part is described by heat conduction. Fourier's law says that heat flux is directed opposite to the temperature gradient and depends on thermal conductivity. The heat equation describes how temperature evolves in time under conduction and a heat source. The mechanical connection appears through thermal expansion. We do not need to read the equations term by term. Their physical meaning is that heat diffuses through the rock, and a temperature increase can produce thermal strain. If the surrounding medium restricts this strain, thermal stress appears.

\KeyPoints
\begin{itemize}
  \item Thermal conductivity controls the heat flux.
  \item The heat equation describes temperature evolution.
  \item Thermal expansion links temperature change to strain.
\end{itemize}

\begin{slidenote}{Mathematical Scope and Assumptions}
\Purpose
Make the simplifications explicit and defensible.

\WhatToSay
The model is formulated as a two-dimensional continuum problem. The displacement field is in-plane, so the model tracks horizontal and vertical displacement components. Small deformation and linear isotropic thermoelasticity are used as first-order approximations. This is a deliberate simplification. The model does not explicitly resolve pores, fractures, fluids, plasticity, damage, or full anisotropic constitutive behavior. These assumptions make the problem tractable for a bachelor-level comparative study and allow the model outputs to be interpreted consistently.

\KeyPoints
\begin{itemize}
  \item The thesis uses a 2D continuum formulation.
  \item Small deformation and linear isotropic thermoelasticity are assumed.
  \item Complex geological effects are acknowledged as limitations.
\end{itemize}

\begin{slidenote}{Directional Prediction}
\Purpose
Define the source--probe query and what the prototype estimates.

\WhatToSay
Directional prediction is built around a source point and a probe point. The vector from the source to the probe defines the direction of interest. From this vector, we compute the distance, unit direction, and azimuth angle. The prototype then estimates the thermal and displacement response associated with that query. The important point is that we are not predicting every possible field value in a complete geological volume. We are estimating directional characteristics for a specified material, geometry, source, probe, and observation time.

\KeyPoints
\begin{itemize}
  \item A source and a probe define the directional query.
  \item Distance and azimuth are explicit geometric quantities.
  \item The predicted response is interpreted along that direction.
\end{itemize}

For each node and time step, the exported outputs include temperature, displacement, velocity, stress, strain, and material features. The purpose of this dataset is to provide a common reference for comparing the surrogate models under the same conditions.

\begin{slidenote}{Data Representation and Preprocessing}
\Purpose

\WhatToSay
The COMSOL CSV exports are aligned into a single node set. Dynamic features are assembled into a spatio-temporal tensor, while static material and geometry features are stored separately. Before training, the feature channels are normalized using the mean and standard deviation. This step is important because the variables have different physical units and scales. The same processed dataset can then support several model types: coordinate-based models, grid-based models, and graph-based models.

\KeyPoints

\begin{slidenote}{PINN as the Physics-Informed Baseline}
\Purpose
Explain why PINN is treated differently from the purely supervised surrogate routes.

\WhatToSay
The PINN route uses a point-wise neural network that takes coordinates, time, and material parameters as input and predicts temperature and displacement components. Its training objective combines supervised loss with residual terms related to velocity, elasticity, and thermal behavior. This should not be interpreted as an exact PDE solution. The careful interpretation is that PINN is a physics-informed baseline: it includes physical structure in the loss and therefore provides a useful reference for the other surrogate approaches.

\KeyPoints
\begin{itemize}
  \item PINN maps coordinates, time, and material parameters to response fields.
  \item The loss includes supervised and physics-related residual terms.
  \item PINN is a physics-informed baseline, not a complete solver.
\end{itemize}

\begin{slidenote}{Threats to Validity}

\textbf{1. Why did you choose directional prediction instead of full-field simulation?}

Because the thesis focuses on a narrower and more defensible research task. Full-field thermoelastic simulation is computationally and physically more complex. Directional prediction allows us to compare source--probe responses, travel time, and model behavior under controlled assumptions.

\textbf{2. Is the prototype validated on real field data?}

No. The current work uses COMSOL-derived synthetic data and analytical sanity checks. The results should be interpreted within simplified modelling assumptions. Field data would be an important next step, but it is outside the current scope.

\textbf{3. Why is PINN called a physics-informed baseline?}

PINN is called a physics-informed baseline because its training objective includes residual terms connected with thermoelastic relations. This means that physical structure is included in the learning objective, but the result is still interpreted as a baseline model inside the simplified prototype.

\textbf{4. Why does FNO perform poorly in the current results?}

The current two-dimensional FNO route has a calibration and output-scale issue. Therefore, it is kept in the comparison as a diagnostic branch. Its architecture is still relevant, but this implementation needs retraining and normalization correction.

\textbf{5. What is the role of the analytical reference $t=r/V_P$?}

It is a simple travel-time sanity check based on distance and P-wave velocity. It is not a complete thermoelastic validation, but it helps identify whether predicted travel times are physically plausible in a basic isotropic approximation.

\textbf{6. What are the main limitations of the work?}

The main limitations are the simplified 2D setting, effective isotropic material parameters, limited synthetic data, fixed-horizon behavior in some surrogate routes, and the absence of direct calibration against real geological measurements.

\textbf{7. What is the main contribution of the diploma work?}

The main contribution is a physically grounded comparative framework for AI-guided directional prediction. It combines thermoelastic theory, geological material parameters, a controlled dataset, and several neural surrogate routes under one consistent comparison workflow.

Good morning. The topic of our diploma work is \textit{AI-Guided Prediction of Thermoelastic Wave Propagation in Geological Media}. The work presents a research prototype for AI-guided directional prediction. It is not a site-calibrated simulator. The goal is to estimate selected directional characteristics of thermoelastic wave propagation under simplified assumptions and to compare several neural surrogate approaches.

The physical motivation is that temperature changes in rocks can create thermal expansion, stress, displacement, and wave-like mechanical response. This is relevant for geothermal systems, underground engineering, and geophysical interpretation. A full coupled numerical simulation can be expensive when many materials and source--probe directions must be compared. Therefore, the thesis formulates a narrower prediction task.

The model uses effective geological material parameters such as density, elastic moduli, thermal conductivity, heat capacity, thermal expansion, and P-wave and S-wave velocities. These parameters describe how different rock types respond mechanically and thermally. The simplified formulation is two-dimensional, uses small deformation and linear isotropic thermoelasticity, and does not explicitly resolve pores, fluids, fractures, plasticity, or full anisotropy.

Directional prediction is defined by a source point and a probe point. Their difference gives the direction, distance, and azimuth. The prototype estimates temperature, displacement, travel time, and directional response for that query. The data are based on a controlled COMSOL-derived two-dimensional thermoelastic setup, exported and normalized for several model families.

The comparison includes PINN, FNO, MeshGraphNet, and a Transformer-style route. PINN is used as the physics-informed baseline because its objective includes thermoelastic residual terms. FNO, MeshGraphNet, and Transformer-style models are treated as surrogate routes for comparison, not as physically equivalent numerical solvers.

The experiments use forty scenarios per material, four materials, and four model routes, giving six hundred forty model responses. In the travel-time comparison, model-family differences are larger than the material difference in the current prototype. Against the simple analytical reference $t=r/V_P$, PINN shows the best agreement and remains within the ten percent band in the balanced sweep. Transformer is the fastest route, while MeshGraphNet and FNO need additional development for time behavior and calibration.

The main limitations are the limited synthetic dataset, the simplified 2D assumptions, the current FNO normalization issue, and the fact that the analytical reference is only a sanity check. The conclusion is that the work provides a physically grounded comparative framework for AI-guided directional prediction within simplified assumptions. Future work should recalibrate FNO, improve time-conditioned MeshGraphNet behavior, extend material data, and compare against additional analytical or numerical baselines.
